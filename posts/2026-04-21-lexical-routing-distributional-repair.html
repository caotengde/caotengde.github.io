Lexical Routing, Distributional Repair, and a Recursive-Depth Residue

A Pre-Committed Twenty-Three-Attempt Sweep of Small Transformers on COGS

Draft — not yet formatted for submission.

1. Introduction

Architectural interventions for compositional generalization in small transformers are difficult to interpret from aggregate accuracy alone. On COGS, SCAN, and related benchmarks, a single Gen number can rise because a model learned lexical copying, because a held-out syntactic position became covered by the training distribution, or because it genuinely learned a recursive rule. Those are different mechanisms, but they are often reported as the same kind of "compositional generalization" improvement.

This paper imposes a deliberately narrow protocol on that problem. We use COGS as the benchmark, keep a small Transformer seq2seq family as the base model, and require every intervention to register numerical thresholds before training. Each attempt has a plan.md written before the run, a findings.md written after the run, and a verdict recorded in a public ledger. Post-hoc interpretation is allowed, but it is explicitly marked as post-hoc and can be overturned by later pre-committed controls.

Across twenty-three attempts, the benchmark decomposes into three distinct failure modes.

First, encoder representation is not the bottleneck for lexical identity. A linear probe on frozen encoders from attempts 03/05/06/07 recovers proper-noun token identity at 99.1-99.4%, while the same models' end-to-end proper-noun Gen accuracy remains near 50-55%. The information is present in the encoder; the decoder fails to route it.

Second, lexical routing is architecturally fixable on the decoder side. A pointer-generator copy head in attempt 08 moves the proper-noun axis by +16.5 percentage points off a seven-attempt plateau. Replacing learned positional embeddings with ALiBi in attempt 09 does not solve the registered structural hypothesis, but it triggers a post-hoc copy-gate regime shift: function-word gates collapse from roughly 0.82 to 0.06, and proper-noun accuracy rises again to 82.2%. These are real wins, but only on lexical routing.

Third, the COGS structural wall is not one wall. The early architectural sweep leaves pp_recursion, cp_recursion, and obj_pp_to_subj_pp at or near zero, and attempt 10 rules out length clipping as the obvious hyperparameter confound. Subsequent pre-committed data, scale, and depth-aware decoder interventions split the wall into three components:

  • obj_pp_to_subj_pp is distributional. It stays at 0% through attempt 13, then rises to 80.4% in attempt 14 after adding only 1600 subject-PP synthetic examples. The fix is stable at 1.2M and 10M parameters.
  • Shallow-to-mid recursion within trained depth support is capacity-sensitive. Coverage scaling alone at 1.2M does not help, but adding targeted synthesis and scaling to 10M lifts pp_recursion from 0.12 to 0.17 with low variance.
  • Deep recursion past roughly depth 6 is an operational decoder problem in this model family. Attempt 18 gives the 10M model direct synthetic training at depths 7-9, yet PP d9 and CP d9 remain exactly 0.000 across all seeds. Attempts 19-23 then rule out explicit input depth features, another 4x scale increase to 32M, decoder self-attention depth bias in both substitutive and additive forms, and bracketed output LFs. Making depth visible in the input, attention mask, or output stream is not enough. The remaining candidate is a decoder with structural operations: tree decoding, stack augmentation, production-rule emission, or a pointer mechanism tied to open modifiers. The contribution is therefore not a state-of-the-art COGS score. It is a pre-committed decomposition. Aggregate Gen hides the fact that lexical routing, cross-position coverage, shallow recursive templates, and deep recursive emission respond to different interventions. The protocol matters because it turns both wins and nulls into evidence: a post-hoc ALiBi win is reported as post-hoc, a length-clipping worry becomes a registered control, and a tempting "just give the model depth" hypothesis is closed by three separate negative interventions.

Contributions

  • A twenty-three-attempt pre-committed COGS sweep, spanning encoder-side factorization, decoder-side copy and positional interventions, targeted data synthesis, scale controls at 1.2M/10M/32M parameters, depth-aware attention variants, and bracketed output targets.
  • A probe-driven pivot showing that proper-noun identity is already linearly recoverable from the encoder, localizing the lexical-routing bottleneck to the decoder.
  • Two decoder-side lexical-routing wins: pointer-generator copy for proper nouns and ALiBi plus copy-gate regime shift for function words.
  • A structural-wall decomposition: cross-position PP transfer is distributional and fixed by 1600 targeted examples; shallow depth recursion is coverage-plus-capacity sensitive; deep recursion remains unsolved under explicit depth supervision, explicit depth features, 32M scale, depth-aware decoder self-attention, and bracketed LF output.
  • A public record of the protocol correcting the research story: post-hoc claims are marked, retractions are preserved, and later pre-committed controls update the interpretation rather than being hidden.

2. Related work

Compositional generalization benchmarks. SCAN [Lake & Baroni 2018] and COGS [Kim & Linzen 2020] are the two most common small-transformer compositional-generalization benchmarks. SCAN's add_prim_jump split has been saturated at ≥98% by CGPS [Li et al. 2019], NeSS [Chen et al. 2020], LANE [Liu et al. 2020], NSR [Nye et al. 2020], Meta-Seq2Seq [Lake 2019], and MLC [Lake & Baroni 2023]; we treated it as a diagnostic rather than a target. COGS is less saturated and maintains non-trivial gaps between IID and Gen across model sizes. gSCAN [Ruis et al. 2020] and CFQ-MCD [Keysers et al. 2020] probe complementary failure modes and are out of scope for this program.

Copy and pointer mechanisms. See et al. [2017] introduced pointer-generator networks for abstractive summarization, combining p_vocab and p_copy via a learned gate. Copy has since been adopted widely for tasks with input/output vocabulary overlap. Its direct application to compositional-generalization benchmarks for proper-noun handling is standard folklore but rarely tested in controlled ablation. Our attempt 08 is a clean registration of that test.

Relative and ALiBi position encodings. Shaw et al. [2018] introduced relative attention biases; Press et al. [2022] (ALiBi) proposed a linear-decay per-head additive bias with no learned parameters. Its primary advertised benefit is length extrapolation. Our attempt 09 registered ALiBi as an intervention for structural generalization (the wrong hypothesis, in retrospect) and recovered a different, unregistered win on function-word routing (Section 6.3).

Structured and tree decoders. Herzig & Berant [2021] introduced a grammar-constrained decoder that achieves strong COGS performance by emitting LF productions rather than flat tokens. Chen et al. [2020] (NeSS) and Nye et al. [2020] (NSR) use explicit symbolic or neuro-symbolic structure. We do not include a full tree, stack, or production-rule decoder in this program. After attempts 19-23 rule out several "make depth visible" interventions, these structural-operation decoders become the main remaining candidate for the deep-recursion residue.

Data-augmentation approaches. GECA [Andreas 2020] synthesizes training examples by composing substructures from the training set, addressing compositional generalization at the data-distribution level rather than the architecture level. Attempts 11-15 test narrow GECA-like synthesis rather than full GECA: depth-tail synthesis for PP/CP recursion, cross-position subject-PP synthesis, and their combination. The contrast between attempts 13 and 14 is central to our decomposition: more depth coverage does not solve deep recursion, while a small targeted cross-position augmentation solves obj_pp_to_subj_pp.

Mechanistic interpretation and probing. Linear probes on hidden states are a standard mechanistic-interpretability tool (Belinkov & Glass 2019; Elazar et al. 2021). Probing as a protocol gate — used to decide whether to continue an architectural research line — appears less often; our attempt-07-to-08 pivot (Section 4) exemplifies that use.

Pre-registered / falsifiable ML research. Pre-registration and pre-committed thresholds are common in medicine and increasingly discussed in ML (Gencoglu et al. 2019; Ulmer et al. 2022). Our protocol is a specific lightweight instantiation: each attempt gets a plan.md file committed before training, and a findings.md file that references it; verdicts are tracked in a public ledger (ATTEMPTS.md). We do not claim originality for the methodology. We claim that running it at twenty-three-attempt depth on one benchmark produces a cleaner decomposition than aggregate-score reporting usually permits.

3. Methodology

3.1 Pre-commit protocol

Every attempt follows the same lightweight pre-commit protocol.

  1. Plan. Before training starts, a plan.md states the hypothesis, intervention, expected failure modes, and numerical thresholds. The pass rule is registered in the plan rather than chosen after results are observed.
  2. Train. A short sanity run checks numerical stability. Full runs use three seeds (42, 43, 44) unless explicitly marked otherwise.
  3. Evaluate. We evaluate IID dev accuracy, full COGS Gen accuracy, all 21 Gen categories, and the registered diagnostic slices for that attempt (for example per-depth PP/CP recursion in attempts 12-23).
  4. Record. A findings.md records observed values against the registered thresholds. Post-hoc interpretation is allowed only if marked as post-hoc; later controls can retract or refine it. This protocol is intentionally modest. Its value is not that it prevents all researcher degrees of freedom, but that it makes the main ones visible: which hypothesis was actually tested, which thresholds were registered, and which later claims were not predicted.

3.2 Benchmark

We use COGS [Kim & Linzen, 2020]. The training split has 24,155 examples. The IID dev split has 3,000 examples. The Gen split has 21,000 examples across 21 categories.

Three Gen categories are central to the paper:

  • cp_recursion: deeper clausal embedding than appears in training.
  • pp_recursion: deeper PP chains than appear in training.
  • obj_pp_to_subj_pp: PP modifiers are seen on object position in training and tested on subject position. The Gen split reaches 61 input tokens and 480 LF tokens. Early attempts used max_in=25 and max_out=150; attempt 10 is the pre-committed control that raises these to 80/500 and rules out truncation as the source of the recursion failures. For aggregate reporting we group categories into four axes: Proper, Common, Structural (pp_recursion, cp_recursion, obj_pp_to_subj_pp), and Other. For the later recursion experiments, aggregate Structural is too coarse, so we report per-depth PP/CP accuracy.

3.5 Probing

Between attempts 07 and 08 we ran a linear probe on frozen encoder hidden states from attempts 03, 05, 06, and 07. For each token position, the probe predicts input token identity from the final encoder state. Proper-noun identity is recoverable at 99.1-99.4% across all variants, including models whose end-to-end proper-noun Gen accuracy is only 50-55%. This result stops the encoder-factorization line and redirects the program to decoder-side interventions.

3.6 What we do not claim

  • We do not claim state-of-the-art COGS performance. The highest aggregate Gen in this program comes from targeted subject-PP synthesis, not a new general architecture.
  • We do not claim that no model can learn deep recursion. Our claim is narrower: in this Transformer seq2seq family, under the registered interventions and scales tested here, deep PP/CP recursion remains at the floor even under direct depth-7-to-9 supervision.
  • We do not claim that pre-commit protocols are sufficient for all ML research. We claim that this protocol made the failure modes legible and prevented post-hoc wins from being confused with registered hypotheses.

4. The probing pivot

After seven attempts (01–07) that applied encoder-side factorization, the proper-noun generalization axis remained stuck at ~50% across every variant. The pattern was consistent: common nouns moved (50% baseline → 87% combo) while proper nouns did not (55% baseline → 50% combo). We needed to know whether the encoder was losing information about proper-noun identity in compositional contexts, or whether the information was present but not being routed through the decoder.

4.1 Setup

We took frozen encoder checkpoints from attempts 03 (baseline), 05 (aux-loss), 06 (combo), and 07 (asymmetric) — three seeds each, twelve checkpoints total. On IID-dev, for every token position, we trained a linear probe to predict input token identity (749-way classification) from the encoder's last-layer hidden state. We broke results out by token class (proper, common, function, structural), holding everything else constant.

4.3 Consequence

This result closed out the encoder-factorization line. We stopped testing encoder-side interventions and pivoted to decoder-side ones:

  • Decoder cross-attention routing (addressed by pointer-generator copy in attempt 08)
  • Decoder output distribution shaping (addressed by ALiBi + gate regime in attempt 09) In retrospect, the probe also explains why attempts 01–07 produced mostly marginal movement. Attempt 05's aux-loss was "first non-null" at +3pp, but the probe suggests the loss was not enforcing a representational structure — it was already satisfied at the encoder. The improvement likely came from a side-effect on decoder attention patterns, not from encoder capacity reallocation. Had we run the probe first, we would have skipped attempts 04, 06, and 07 entirely and saved ~300 GPU-min. The probe is a small experiment (~30 GPU-min, 12 checkpoints, one linear classifier). Its role in the research program was disproportionate: one well-aimed probe redirected every attempt that followed.

5. Win 1 — Pointer-generator copy unblocks proper-noun routing

Attempt 08 added a See et al. (2017) pointer-generator decoder head to the attempt 03 baseline. At each decoder step, the model computes two distributions over the output vocabulary:

  • p_vocab(t): the standard softmax over the output vocabulary
  • p_copy(t): aggregated cross-attention weights placed on input positions whose token is t and combines them via a learned gate g ∈ [0, 1]: p_final(t) = g · p_vocab(t) + (1 − g) · p_copy(t) For COGS the intersection between input and output vocabularies is substantial: 601 of 749 input-vocabulary tokens are also valid output tokens (80.2%). Proper nouns, common nouns, and function words are all copyable; LF operators (AND, x_k, agent, etc.) are not.

5.4 Seed variance

One of three seeds (s44) collapsed to 58–61% on two proper-noun categories while the other two seeds stayed above 95%. The standard deviation on the proper axis is 11.7pp — the worst variance we observed on that axis in the original architectural sweep. The copy mechanism works reliably in most runs but admits a basin of attraction where the gate never learns to favor copy for held-out proper nouns.

5.5 What attempt 08 shows

The copy mechanism is a decoder-side intervention that targets exactly the bottleneck the probe identified: information present in the encoder was not being routed to the decoder output for proper nouns. Copy provides a second path from input to output that bypasses the standard cross-attention → vocab-softmax pipeline. It moves the proper-noun axis by +16.5pp from a seven-attempt plateau.

This is our first win. It does not touch the structural axis (Section 7).

6. Win 2 — ALiBi removes a PE–gate coupling we didn't know existed

Attempt 09 modified the attempt 08 pointer-generator by replacing learned positional embeddings with ALiBi [Press et al. 2022] — a per-head linear additive attention bias −m_h · |i − j| applied before softmax — and removing positional-embedding parameters entirely. Self-attention in both encoder and decoder used ALiBi; cross-attention was left unchanged (no PE between encoder and decoder positions).

Post-hoc inspection of the gate statistics revealed the unregistered win:

Function-word gates collapsed to near-zero under ALiBi — they are now copy-dominant rather than vocab-dominant. This did not happen in attempt 08 even though attempt 08 had the same copy mechanism. Attempt 09's only other change was removing the learned PE and replacing it with ALiBi.

We interpret this as follows. In the learned-PE model, the absolute position of a function word (the usually appears near position 1 or 2, etc.) is entangled with its embedding signal. The vocab-softmax path could predict function words partly from PE, giving it a slight edge that held the gate above 0.8. When ALiBi removed the PE, the vocab path lost that edge, and the gate settled on copy — which is robust to position shifts between training and test. Function words in held-out sentences appear at positions the model has never seen, so copy-from-source is a strictly better policy.

6.4 Attribution of the +4.6pp aggregate lift

Attempt 09's +4.6pp Gen improvement over attempt 08 decomposes approximately as:

  • Proper axis +15.8pp × 4/21 categories = +3.0pp contribution to Gen
  • Function-word-driven improvement on "Other" categories (mostly prim_to_inf_arg +4pp and compositional-generalization contexts where function-word copy matters) ≈ +2pp
  • Common axis regression and continued structural-gen failure: ≈ −0.4pp net The function-word effect is not isolated to any single "Other" category — it shows up as a distributed ~0.5pp lift on several.

6.5 Interpretation

What we registered as a structural-generalization hypothesis became, in practice, a lexical-routing win on a token class we had not been thinking about. The pre-commit protocol marked it REJECTED, correctly: we did not achieve what we claimed we would. The post-hoc story in this section is our best current account of what did happen, but it is not a pre-registered finding. We flag this clearly because it illustrates the protocol's intended role — separating what the evidence supports from what we wish it supported.

A subsequent attempt that pre-registers "ALiBi makes the gate copy-dominant for function words" would be a cleaner test. We did not run that attempt.

6.6 What attempt 09 shows

Two decoder-side interventions now yield two orthogonal lexical-routing wins:

  • Attempt 08 (copy mechanism): primarily helps proper-noun generalization. Proper axis 50% → 66%.
  • Attempt 09 (ALiBi interacting with the existing copy gate): additionally helps function-word routing. Proper axis 66% → 82%; Gen 64% → 69%. Neither attempt moves the structural axis — the subject of Section 7.

This observation raised a serious confound: was the "wall" an architectural phenomenon, or a hyperparameter one? Under the pre-commit protocol we registered attempt 10 as a specific control: re-run the attempt 09 architecture with max_in = 80 and max_out = 500 (covering gen-split max_src = 61 and max_lf = 480 with headroom), change nothing else. If the wall is a clipping artifact, pp_recursion and cp_recursion should move; obj_pp_to_subj_pp should remain unchanged because its inputs never clipped.

7.3 What we think is happening

Our initial account of the wall is distributional but non-uniform across the three categories. COGS training LFs have a maximum of 153 tokens (about 8 atomic predicates, recursion depth ≤ 2). The Gen split tests LFs up to 480 tokens (up to 43 predicates, depth 5+). For the depth categories (pp_recursion, cp_recursion), the model never observes a training example in which the recursive production fires more than twice. For obj_pp_to_subj_pp, the failure is cross-positional: a PP modifier trained only on object positions is asked to appear in subject position at test time — a different failure mode even though it coexists at 0% aggregate.

This distinction turned out to be crucial. §7.5 (attempts 11–13) shows that for the depth categories, coverage is necessary but not sufficient: 800 examples per depth lift in-support depths partially, but 2500 per depth cannot lift the ceiling. §7.6 (attempt 14) shows that for obj_pp_to_subj_pp, coverage is both necessary and sufficient: 1600 targeted synthetic examples close the gap from 0% to 80%. The two categories fail for mechanistically different reasons.

This is consistent with:

  • The probe result (Section 4): encoder has the information. The missing piece is in the decoder output distribution.
  • The flat 0% across categorically different early interventions (encoder factorization, typed routing, copy, ALiBi, raised lengths). An architectural-capacity hypothesis that implicates a specific component should have shown something across the first ten COGS attempts. The fact that a small targeted data intervention (a14, 1600 examples) moved obj_pp_to_subj_pp while no architectural change did is the strongest evidence that the initial 0% on that category was data-distribution-driven, not architectural-capacity-driven. The literature agrees directionally on depth: tree-structured decoders (Herzig & Berant 2021; Chen et al. 2020), data-augmentation approaches (GECA, Andreas 2020), and curriculum methods have crossed the depth categories where architectural changes alone have not. We did not include decoder-structural interventions in this program.

7.4 What the wall does and does not rule out

The program now rules out several explanations as sufficient:

  1. Encoder representation. Attempts 04-07 and the linear probe show that lexical identity is already present in the encoder.
  2. Typed attention or auxiliary factorization. These move some common noun categories but do not solve proper nouns or structural recursion.
  3. Copy and ALiBi alone. Attempts 08-09 produce lexical-routing wins, but leave the structural categories at the floor.
  4. Length limits. Attempt 10 raises max input/output lengths and leaves recursion unchanged.
  5. Depth coverage at fixed 1.2M scale. Attempts 11-13 show partial in-support movement, but 3.1× more depth data does not flatten the depth-decay curve.
  6. Cross-position capacity. Attempt 14 solves obj_pp_to_subj_pp with 1600 examples, so that category was data-distributional rather than an architectural capacity limit.
  7. Scale as a simple fix. Attempt 17 shows useful 10M gains inside trained depth support; attempt 20 shows that the response does not continue monotonically to 32M and does not lift d7-d9.
  8. Making depth visible. Attempts 19, 21, 22, and 23 expose depth at the input, in decoder self-attention, and in the output stream. None lifts past-d6 recursion. The program still does not test a full structural-operation decoder. Tree decoders, stack-augmented decoders, production-rule decoders, and pointer-to-open-modifier mechanisms remain viable follow-up candidates. The narrower claim is that the ordinary linearized Transformer decoder, even with copy, ALiBi, explicit depth signals, bracketed outputs, and up to 32M parameters, does not learn a robust recursive emission operation for COGS PP/CP depth.

The nine-way wall decomposition (a13 + a17 + a18 + a19 + a20 + a21 + a22 + a23, plus a10)

Five attempts now jointly pin down what kind of phenomenon the depth wall is:

  • a13: at 1.2M, 3.1× coverage at d3..d6 does not lift any depth's accuracy. Rules out: "coverage of trained depths is the bottleneck."
  • a17: at 10M with the same 800/d coverage as a15, in-support accuracy lifts substantially (PP d5 0.03→0.23, d6 0.00→0.05), but d7+ stays at 0. Rules out: "scale alone lifts radius past trained depths."
  • a18: at 10M with 800/d coverage extended to d7..d9, in-support accuracy at the newly-trained deep depths is 0.00-0.02, and the d9 number is 0.000 across all three seeds. Rules out: "in-support coverage lifts deep depths."
  • a19: at 10M with the a18 12800-synth mix, add a learned per-token depth feature (nn.Embedding(16, 256) summed with the token embedding, depth_id[i] = count of {in, on, beside, that} strictly before position i). PP d7 stays at 0.017 — identical to a18 — and PP d4–d6 regress by 5–15pp. Rules out: "past-d6 failure is an input-representation gap."
  • a20: at 32M (4× a17 scale; d_model 256→512, heads 8→16), with the a18 12800-synth mix and no depth embedding, PP d7 lands at 0.013 (worse than a18), PP d9 stays at 0.000, and in-support depths regress 15–27pp (PP d4 0.54→0.28, d5 0.21→0.06). Rules out: "more scale crosses past-d6" — the log-linear extrapolation from a15→a17 (which predicted PP d5 ≥ 0.40 and PP d7 ≥ 0.06) is falsified at the next multiplier. The remaining conclusion: a sequence-to-sequence transformer with pointer-generator copy, tested at 1.2M, 10M, and 32M parameters (27× total scale range), learns depth-recursion as a bag of shallow structural templates — not a recursive rule. At depths past roughly 6, there is too little frequent-enough structural redundancy in the gen split for this template-extraction mechanism to recognize a deeper instance, even when 800 explicit training examples at that depth are supplied, even when each input token carries an explicit learned depth label, and even when the parameter budget is raised another 4×. The bottleneck is therefore not at the encoder, the input level, or the scale-of-parameters axis within the regimes we have tested — it lives in the decoder's inability to compose recursive output structure. This decomposes the paper's three-category "structural wall" into three mechanistically distinct phenomena:
  1. obj_pp_to_subj_pp — fully distributional. 1600 targeted examples close it at 1.2M; scale-invariant (a14, a17, a20 all cluster near 0.80–0.84).
  2. pp_recursion / cp_recursion within depth support — capacity-bound below a ceiling. Scale 1.2M → 10M multiplies d5-d6 in-support accuracy by 7-17× (a12 → a17). Scale 10M → 32M at the same step budget regresses all trained depths (a20), so the capacity lift is not a pure log-linear scaling law but has a ceiling at or near the 10M/22k optimum.
  3. pp_recursion / cp_recursion at deeper-than-templatable depths and past all support — architectural, decoder-side. Neither coverage (a13), scale alone (a17), their combination at d7..d9 with explicit supervision (a18), an input-side learned depth feature (a19), a 4× further scale increase to 32M (a20), a decoder- self-attention head-replacement with a depth-distance bias (a21), the additive formulation of that same decoder-attention intervention with a learnable scalar α (a22), nor a visible depth register wrapped around every nmod/ccomp subtree in the output stream (a23) lifts this. a19 localizes the architectural residue downstream of the encoder; a20 rules out scale as a fix within this transformer backbone family; a21 rules out substitutive decoder-self-attention depth bias; a22 rules out additive decoder-self-attention depth bias — the model actively uses the depth signal (learned α drifts 20% upward from init across all seeds) and yet PP d9 stays at 0.000 and PP d7 at the a18 floor of 0.010; a23 rules out the minimum-invasive output-structural intervention — making depth visible as a surface token on the output stream regresses in-support depth recursion by up to 32pp (PP d3 0.867 → 0.543) without lifting past-d6, because emitting the bracket at the right moment is itself a structural learning problem. Decoder self-attention attending to its own emitted prefix with a depth-distance signal is not the missing ingredient, and neither is a visible output-side depth register. All three components are supported by at least one pre-committed experiment with an explicit falsification criterion. The third component is the strongest architectural null the program has produced: d9 = 0.000 under targeted supervision is not a coverage artifact, not an input-representation artifact, not a scale artifact across 27× parameter range, and not a depth-visibility artifact in the input (a19), in self-attention (a21+a22), or in the output stream (a23).

7.7 Why the null is a contribution

COGS has been a touchstone benchmark for compositional-generalization claims in small transformers since 2020. Many architectural papers report aggregate Gen improvements without per-category breakdowns or pre-committed thresholds. Our sweep shows that the aggregate metric hides a sharper decomposition: some categories are architecturally fixable with small decoder-side changes (proper nouns, function words), one apparently structural category is fixed by a tiny targeted data intervention (obj_pp_to_subj_pp), shallow recursion inside trained support is helped by synthesis plus scale, and deep recursive emission remains at the floor under a long list of controls. A paper that reports only aggregate Gen cannot distinguish these regimes. A pre-committed, per-category protocol can.

8. Discussion

8.1 Three decompositions from one benchmark

This program's interventions yield three nested decompositions of COGS generalization, each confirmed by an explicit pre-committed experiment.

(a) Aggregate vs per-category. Aggregate Gen numbers conflate distinct failure modes. Our per-category pre-committed protocol distinguishes (i) lexical-routing wins, (ii) already-solvable "Other" category improvements, and (iii) actual movement on the wall categories — all of which can produce similar aggregate lifts.

(b) Lexical routing vs structural extrapolation. Lexical-routing failures (which input token is routed to which output position?) respond to standard decoder-side architectural changes — pointer-generator copy mechanism and relative positional encoding. Structural-extrapolation failures (generate a structure not seen at training) do not respond to any of the five architectural interventions we tested, and a pre-committed length-clipping control rules out the most obvious alternative explanation.

(c) Three-way decomposition of the structural wall. The three "wall" categories are not one phenomenon. Three pre-committed experiments jointly isolate three mechanisms.

Cross-position failure (obj_pp_to_subj_pp) is fully distributional. Attempt 14 (1600 subject-PP examples) lifts this category from flat 0% to 80.4% mean in a single pre-committed shot at 1.2M params; attempt 17 holds it at 0.84 at 10M — scale-invariant.

Depth-axis accuracy within trained support (d3..d6) is capacity-bound. Attempt 13 (20000 examples, coverage scaling at 1.2M) does not lift any depth. Attempt 17 (8× scale at fixed coverage) multiplies each depth's in-support accuracy by 7-17× relative to a12. Coverage alone does not help; scale does.

Depth-axis accuracy past d6 — both out-of-support and even in-support when trained at d7..d9 — is architectural. Attempt 18 provides 800 training examples each at depths d7, d8, and d9 on the same 10M backbone, and gets d9 = 0.000 across all three seeds for both PP and CP axes. d7 and d8 extract only 0.010-0.020 from their 800 examples. This is the strongest architectural null in the program: direct supervision at a specific depth produces no gen-set lift at that depth.

The architectural bottleneck is not an input-representation gap. Attempt 19 is a pre-committed control that directly tests whether the past-d6 failure is caused by the encoder lacking an explicit per-token depth signal. We add a single learned Embedding(16, 256) summed with the input token embedding, with depth_id[i] set to the count of {in, on, beside, that} tokens strictly before position i. The rest of the architecture (ALiBi, pointer-generator), the 10M backbone, the 22k-step schedule, and the 12800-line a18 synth are held fixed. PP d7 stays at 0.017, PP d9 stays at 0.000 — identical to a18 within noise. Unexpectedly, in-support depths regress (PP d4 0.54 → 0.39, d5 0.21 → 0.06, d6 0.06 → 0.02): the model uses the depth index as a shortcut feature for memorizing training templates rather than learning a more compositional mapping. The cross-position axis is retained (obj_pp_to_subj_pp 0.78) and IID accuracy is preserved, so the effect is specific to depth, not a general-training failure. a19 therefore closes out the natural next confound — that the transformer simply lacked a clean depth signal — and narrows the remaining architectural candidate to decoder-side recursive composition.

The architectural bottleneck is also not crossed by another 4× in scale. Attempt 20 pushes parameters from 10M to 32M (d_model 256 → 512, heads 8 → 16) while holding the a18 synth, schedule, and architecture (ALiBi + pointer-generator, no depth embedding) fixed. The log-linear extrapolation from a15 (1.2M) → a17 (10M) — which predicted pp_recursion ≈ 0.29 and PP d5 ≈ 0.43 at 32M — is falsified on every wall metric. PP d7 lands at 0.013 (below a18's 0.017), PP d9 at 0.000, in-support depths regress by 15–27pp (PP d4 0.54 → 0.28, d5 0.21 → 0.06). Combined with a19 this demonstrates that within the transformer-sequence-to-sequence family and the scale range we tested (1.2M, 10M, 32M — 27× total range), past-d6 failure is not scale-bound; the capacity lift from 1.2M to 10M hits a ceiling at the 10M/22k point rather than continuing as a smooth scaling law.

The architectural bottleneck is also not fixed by either a substitutive or an additive decoder-self-attention depth bias. Attempts 21 and 22 are the program's two decoder-side architectural interventions on the past-d6 wall. On the same 10M backbone and the a18 12800-line synth, we replace head 0's ALiBi position-distance bias (head 0 is the smallest-slope and therefore the longest-range ALiBi head) with a depth-distance bias using the count of nmod+ccomp output tokens as the depth signal. The 7 other heads are left intact. Parameter count is identical to a18. The past-d6 axes are not lifted (PP d7 = 0.010, PP d9 = 0.000 — essentially the same floor as a18), and in-support depths modestly regress: PP d3 falls 0.867 → 0.797, d4 0.543 → 0.443, d5 0.207 → 0.133. Per- seed d3 is [0.91, 0.61, 0.87] — seed 43 is a visible outlier and the regression is closer to a18-parity for seeds 42 and 44. The effect size is within the program's familiar lateral-variance range. The mechanism we originally proposed — that head 0's shallow slope was a load-bearing long-range continuity channel whose substitution collapsed in-support accuracy — is not supported by the corrected numbers (see retraction note in §7). The milder interpretation is that replacing head 0's position bias with a depth bias loses a small amount of useful positional signal without recovering generalization past d6. obj_pp_to_subj_pp is retained at 0.853 (program best) because its tests are short. a21 therefore does not rule out the broader "decoder-side depth-aware attention" hypothesis on its own, only the substitution implementation of it.

Attempt 22 tests the additive formulation. All 8 ALiBi heads retain their position bias; head 0 additionally receives a learnable −α · |depth(i) − depth(j)| term with α initialized to head 0's ALiBi slope. Parameter count is a18 + 1. Pre-committed thresholds match a21's (P1 PP d7 ≥ 0.10, P2 PP d9 ≥ 0.03, P3 pp_rec ≥ 0.25, S1 PP d3 ≥ 0.75 as a direct falsifier of "additive preserves positional bandwidth," S2 PP d5 ≥ 0.18, R1/R2/R3 retention). a22 is REJECTED 3/8 by the same P1/P2/P3 triad, and its per-seed split is instructive. Seeds 43 and 44 sit at PP d3 0.89/0.91, at or above a18's 0.867; seed 42 sits at 0.31 with IID 0.987 (the other two at 0.997/0.998). For 2 of 3 seeds additive depth bias does exactly what the plan predicted — preserves in-support accuracy while not helping past-d6 — and for the third the model finds a degraded optimum. Crucially, the final learned α per seed is [0.605, 0.602, 0.578], all above the 0.5 initialization. The clean-null prediction pre-registered in the a22 plan ("if depth is useless, α learns toward 0") is decisively refuted: the model actively uses the depth signal, and past-d6 stays at 0 anyway. Combined with a21, decoder self-attention equipped with a depth-distance bias under either substitutive or additive formulations fails to cross past-d6.

Attempt 23 tests the minimum-invasive output-structural intervention that follows naturally from the a21+a22 rule-out: do not modify how the decoder attends, modify what it emits. Target LFs are rewritten so every nmod/ccomp subtree is wrapped in [...]; the unclosed-bracket count at any emission position is then exactly the current nesting depth. Two tokens are added to the output vocabulary and the parameter budget is a18 + 0.01%. Under the hypothesis that past-d6 failure reflects an absent visible depth register, a23 should lift past-d6; under the null it should be neutral or mildly harmful. a23 is REJECTED 3/8: P1 fails at PP d7 = 0.010, P2 fails at PP d9 = 0.000, P3 fails at pp_rec = 0.092 (program worst). More informatively, calibration thresholds S1 (PP d3 ≥ 0.75) and S2 (PP d5 ≥ 0.18) also fail, with PP d3 dropping 32pp below a18 (0.867 → 0.543) and d4/d5 dropping 26/13pp. Two of three seeds collapse at d3 ([0.38, 0.91, 0.34]). Bracketing is actively harmful in-support because the decoder must learn to place [ and ] at the correct positions — making depth visible on the output stream does not sidestep the structural learning problem, it reifies it. Combined with a19 (depth visible at input) and a21+a22 (depth visible in attention), the three "make depth visible" interventions jointly fail. The architectural residue is not in how the decoder attends over its own emitted prefix, nor in what tokens the decoder emits; the remaining candidate is the decoder's structural operations for emitting recursive output — tree decoding, stack augmentation, production-rule emission, or pointer-copy-to-open-modifier.

Two sibling data-side interventions at the same backbone (a13 scaling up d3..d6 coverage, a14 cross-position synthesis) produce opposite pre-committed outcomes. A scale-matched follow-up (a17) cleanly attributes in-support depth-accuracy to capacity up to a ceiling between 10M and 32M (a20). A coverage-matched depth-extension (a18) cleanly attributes past-d6 failure to architecture. An input-side architectural control (a19) cleanly attributes the past-d6 architectural residue to decoder-side composition rather than input representation. A scale-follow-up (a20) rules out that the residue lives above 10M parameters. The original COGS three-category "structural" grouping merged categories with three mechanistically different failure modes.

8.3 Why the protocol changes the result

The most consequential moment in this program was between attempts 07 and 08. Attempts 03–07 had produced steady +3pp aggregate improvements on the common axis while proper-axis performance oscillated around 50%. Under a conventional protocol, the next step would have been "attempt 08: more encoder capacity" or "attempt 08: combine 05 and 07 more carefully." Instead we ran a 30-GPU-minute linear probe, observed the 99%-vs-50% gap, and redirected the entire program to decoder-side interventions. That redirect produced both the program's two wins (attempts 08, 09). A protocol that forbade the probing diagnostic — or that treated it as optional — would almost certainly have spent the rest of our budget on encoder variants at diminishing returns.

8.4 Why the protocol also rejected our best attempt

Attempt 09 is formally REJECTED by the pre-commit protocol: 3 of 6 registered criteria passed; the three that failed were the ones we registered as our core claim. The function-word gate collapse (which now looks like the paper's most interesting finding) was a post-hoc observation. Under the protocol, we report the failure of the registered hypothesis first, flag the post-hoc story separately, and do not conflate them. This is the cost of the methodology: even when you uncover an interesting effect, you have to admit that you did not predict it, and call that attempt rejected.

8.5 Scope and scale caveats

The program is still small-model research. Most attempts use a 1.2M or 10M parameter Transformer; one control reaches 32M. Published systems with much larger models, tree-structured decoders, neuro-symbolic components, or broader data augmentation can reach substantially higher COGS scores. We therefore do not claim that deep recursion is impossible in general. The claim is narrower: within this linearized Transformer seq2seq family, and under the registered interventions tested here, PP/CP recursion past roughly depth 6 remains at the floor even with direct synthetic supervision and explicit depth visibility.

The small scale is also what makes the protocol feasible. Each attempt costs roughly one to three H100-hours across three seeds, so the program can afford many falsifiable attempts instead of a single expensive run.

This attempt rules out the input-representation-gap hypothesis: the encoder already has access to the depth signal (via in/on/beside/that tokens through self-attention), and providing a cleaner learned copy cannot lift generalization past d6. The remaining architectural candidate is decoder-side recursive composition.
  10. Attempt 20 (4× further scale, pure scale ablation). Same a18 architecture (ALiBi + pointer-generator, no depth embedding), same 12800-synth mix, same 22k-step schedule. Backbone scaled from 10M to 32M (d_model 256 → 512, heads 8 → 16, head_dim 32 unchanged, layers 4+4, batch 48 — reduced from 64 to fit 80GB H100). Actual params 31,082,712. Eight pre-committed thresholds with PP d7 ≥ 0.10 (decisive) and PP d7 ≥ 0.03 (soft) as H1-vs- null tests, driven by the log-linear extrapolation from a15 → a17. REJECTED on primary (3/8): PP d7 = 0.013, PP d9 = 0.000, pp_recursion aggregate 0.110 (below a18's 0.171); in-support depths regress 15–27pp (PP d4 0.54 → 0.28, d5 0.21 → 0.06). R1/R2/R3 (cross-position ≥ 0.75, gen_agg ≥ 0.55, iid ≥ 0.99) pass, but seed 43 IID falls to 0.981, signaling capacity strain. The log-linear extrapolation from a15/a17 (which predicted pp_recursion ≈ 0.29 and PP d5 ≈ 0.43) is falsified: the scale response between 10M and 32M is not a continuation of the scale response between 1.2M and 10M. Interpretation favors undertraining at batch 48 combined with capacity-reallocation compounded at 32M: the larger model spends capacity on memorizing finer-grained training templates rather than extracting deeper structure. This attempt is the program's fifth pre-committed rule-out — past-d6 failure is not crossed by coverage (a13), scale alone (a17), coverage-of-deep-support (a18), input-side depth feature (a19), or 4× more scale at the extended-depth regime (a20). The architectural residue is localized to decoder-side recursive composition, within the transformer-sequence-to-sequence family at 1.2M–32M parameters (27× total scale range).
  11. Attempt 21 (decoder self-attention depth-aware, head-replacement). Same 10M backbone, same a18 12800-line synth, same 22k schedule. Architectural intervention (first decoder-side intervention on the past-d6 wall): replace the decoder self-attention's ALiBi position-distance bias on head 0 — the smallest-slope, longest-range ALiBi head — with a depth-distance bias, where depth at each decode position is the count of nmod+ccomp output tokens emitted so far. The remaining 7 heads are unchanged; parameter count is identical to a18. Eight pre-committed thresholds with PP d7 ≥ 0.10 (decisive), PP d9 ≥ 0.03, and pp_recursion ≥ 0.25 as the decisive criteria. REJECTED on primary (3/8): all three primaries fail. Mild in-support regression: PP d3 0.867 → 0.797, d4 0.543 → 0.443, d5 0.207 → 0.133; past-d6 essentially unchanged (d7 0.010, d9 0.000). Per-seed d3 is [0.91, 0.61, 0.87] — seed 43 is a visible outlier and the effect is closer to a18-parity for seeds 42 and 44. obj_pp_to_subj_pp is retained at 0.853 (program best) because its tests are short. The original findings (filed same day) reported catastrophic regression (d3 → 0.183); that was a train/eval bug — the intervention's activation flag was a plain Python attribute not restored by load_state_dict, so eval silently ran standard ALiBi on weights trained with depth bias. The corrected numbers show a modest perturbation rather than a collapse, with the same REJECTED verdict (P1/P2/P3 all still fail). This rules out the substitution formulation of decoder-side depth-aware self-attention but does not rule out the broader hypothesis: additive formulations (add depth-bias as an extra term on head 0, or add a 9th head with depth bias) remain untested and are the natural a22.
  12. Attempt 22 (decoder self-attention depth-aware, additive formulation). Same 10M backbone, same a18 12800-line synth, same 22k schedule. All 8 ALiBi heads retain their position bias; head 0 additionally receives −α · |depth(i) − depth(j)| with α a single learnable scalar initialized to head 0's ALiBi slope. Parameter count = a18 + 1 scalar. Eight pre-committed thresholds with P1 PP d7 ≥ 0.10 (decisive), P2 PP d9 ≥ 0.03, P3 pp_rec ≥ 0.25 as decisive; S1 PP d3 ≥ 0.75 as direct falsifier of "additive preserves positional bandwidth"; S2 PP d5 ≥ 0.18; R1/R2/R3 retention. REJECTED on primary (3/8): P1/P2/P3 all fail. Per-depth PP means: d3 0.703, d4 0.393, d5 0.157, d6 0.013, d7 0.010, d9 0.000. Per-seed d3 is [0.31, 0.89, 0.91]; seed 42 is a clear outlier (IID 0.987 vs 0.998/0.997 for the others), and for seeds 43/44 a22 sits at or above a18-parity on PP d3. Learned α per seed is [0.605, 0.602, 0.578] — a consistent 20% upward drift from the 0.5 initialization, decisively refuting the clean-null prediction ("if depth is useless, α → 0"): the model actively uses the depth signal. Despite that, PP d7 = 0.010, PP d9 = 0.000, identical to a18/a19/a20/a21. Past-d6 is invariant under decoder self-attention depth bias in either formulation. Combined with a21, decoder-self-attention depth-bias interventions — substitutive AND additive — fail to crack the past-d6 wall. Seven pre-committed decoder/input/scale/coverage rule-outs now stand (a13, a17, a18, a19, a20, a21, a22); the remaining architectural candidate is decoder-output- structural.
  13. Attempt 23 (bracketed-LF output, decoder-output-structural minimum-invasive). Same 10M backbone, same a18 12800-line synth, same 22k schedule. Target LFs are rewritten so every nmod/ ccomp subtree is wrapped in [...]: the unclosed-bracket count at any emission position is exactly the current nesting depth. Output vocabulary grows by 2 tokens (727 → 729); total parameter count = 8,070,873 (a18 + 0.01%). No attention change. Eight pre-committed thresholds identical in spirit to a21/a22. REJECTED on primary (3/8): P1 (PP d7 ≥ 0.10) fails at 0.010; P2 (d9 ≥ 0.03) fails at 0.000; P3 (pp_rec ≥ 0.25) fails at 0.092 (program worst on this axis). Calibration S1 (PP d3 ≥ 0.75) fails at 0.543 and S2 (PP d5 ≥ 0.18) fails at 0.077; R1 (obj_pp 0.850), R2 (gen_agg 0.662), and R3 (iid 0.995) pass. Per-depth PP d3 [0.38, 0.91, 0.34] — two of three seeds collapse at d3. The result is decisive in a direction we did not pre-expect: bracketing is not neutral but actively harmful in-support (PP d3 −32pp, d4 −26pp, d5 −13pp vs a18), because emitting [ and ] at the right positions is itself an output-structural learning problem that making depth visible does not sidestep. Past-d6 is unchanged (PP d7/d8/d9 = 0.010/0.010/ 0.000, identical to a18–a22). a23 closes the minimum-invasive output-structural intervention. Making depth visible in input (a19), in self-attention (a21 + a22), or in output (a23) all fail — the architectural residue is not representational but operational, requiring structural decoder mechanisms (tree, stack, production-rule, pointer-to-open-modifier) rather than a depth signal to attend to or emit. The data-side findings jointly establish that the apparent three-category "structural wall" is two mechanistically distinct phenomena: a depth-axis wall that is coverage×scale-sensitive within trained depths (neither coverage alone via a13 nor scale alone would move it; their combination lifts pp_recursion along the trajectory 0.09 → 0.12 → 0.19 across a12/a15/a16/a17, but the trajectory stalls and regresses at 32M in a20 — the capacity lift has a ceiling at or near the 10M/22k point), and a cross-position wall that is purely distributional and already saturated at the 1.2M-parameter regime (800–1600 targeted examples close it entirely; 8× and 32× scale both leave it unchanged). The two interventions compose super-additively on wall axes. Further progress on the depth axes' post-training-distribution transfer (generalization past the deepest trained depth) remains open: modal-decay radius stays at ≈0 steps beyond trained depth at 10M and 32M. Broader-scope augmentation (full GECA) or decoder-side architectural changes targeting explicit structural operations (tree, stack, production-rule, or pointer-to-open- modifier decoding) are the remaining candidates. The cross-position axis is effectively closed by 1600 targeted synthesis examples at any scale we have tested.

8.7 What we still have not run, and would

Two follow-ons would further refine the story:

  1. Structural-operation decoders (tree, stack, production-rule, pointer-to-open-modifier). a19 rules out the input-representation gap; a21 and a22 jointly rule out decoder self-attention depth bias in either substitutive or additive formulation; a23 rules out the minimum-invasive output-structural intervention — a visible surface-token depth register regresses in-support depth recursion by up to 32pp without lifting past-d6, because emitting the bracket at the right moment is itself a structural learning problem. Across three "make depth visible" interventions (input embedding, attention bias, output token), the hypothesis that past-d6 is representational is decisively refuted: the architectural residue is operational. The remaining candidates all equip the decoder with explicit structural operations rather than a depth signal to attend to:
  • Tree decoder. Emit nodes with explicit left/right child pointers; subtree nesting is a first-class decoding operation.
  • Stack-augmented decoder. Explicit push/pop operations on a differentiable stack whose state is part of the hidden representation; nmod/ccomp marker tokens gate push, and subtree closure gates pop.
  • Production-rule decoder. Emit grammar productions rather than surface tokens; tree structure is implicit in the production sequence, and each production's applicability is constrained by the partial tree.
  • Pointer-copy-to-open-modifier. Pointer-generator variant where the copy distribution attends not only to input tokens but to previously-emitted open modifiers awaiting closure. Implementation cost for any of these is substantial and the intervention departs from the small-transformer baseline, so we flag it as follow-on work.
  1. Pre-registered function-word intervention (ALiBi + a gate regularizer that explicitly encourages copy for function words). This would convert the attempt-09 post-hoc finding into a pre-registered one. We flag these as future work for the same reason as before: running them without registration would undermine the protocol that is the paper's central methodological commitment.

9. Conclusion

We ran a twenty-three-attempt pre-committed program on COGS. The resulting picture is not "architecture works" or "architecture fails." It is a decomposition.

Encoder-side factorization was the wrong locus for lexical identity: proper-noun identity was already recoverable from the encoder at 99%. Decoder-side copy then produced the first proper-noun routing win, and ALiBi plus copy produced an unregistered but repeatable-looking function-word routing shift. Those are lexical-routing effects, not structural recursion.

The structural categories split further. obj_pp_to_subj_pp was a distributional hole and was largely fixed by 1600 targeted subject-PP examples. PP/CP recursion inside trained depth support moved only when targeted synthesis was paired with 10M-scale capacity. PP/CP recursion past roughly depth 6 remained at the floor under direct d7-d9 supervision, explicit input depth features, 32M scale, decoder depth-attention biases, and bracketed output LFs.

The remaining failure is therefore operational rather than representational: the linearized Transformer decoder does not acquire a robust recursive emission operation from these interventions. The next serious tests are not more ways to mark depth, but decoders that manipulate structure directly: tree, stack, production-rule, or pointer-to-open-modifier decoders.
